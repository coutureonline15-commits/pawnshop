Frequently Asked Questions

Common pawnshop customer questions and answers

A pawn is a short-term, collateral-based loan. You bring in an item of value, the pawnbroker assesses its worth, and then offers you a loan based on a percentage of that value. The item serves as collateral. You get your item back once you repay the loan amount plus interest/fees by the agreed-upon due date.

Pawning means you take out a loan using your item as collateral, and you intend to pay back the loan to reclaim your item. Selling means you transfer ownership of the item to the pawnshop immediately in exchange for cash, and you cannot retrieve the item later.

The pawnbroker assesses the item based on its current market value (what it would sell for used), its condition, and the demand for that specific item. They generally loan a percentage of the estimated resale value to protect their investment.

Loan amounts vary widely but are typically a percentage of the item's estimated resale value (often 25% to 60%). The exact amount depends on the item, its condition, and the pawnshop's policies.

Loan periods vary by state regulation, but are typically 30 days, sometimes with a grace period. Customers usually have options to renew or extend the loan by paying the accrued interest and fees.

If you choose not to pay back the loan or renew it by the due date, the pawnshop forfeits (takes legal ownership of) your collateral and may sell it to recover the money loaned. There are typically no legal consequences or negative impact on your credit score.

Reputable pawnshops securely store and insure all collateral. Your item is typically kept in a safe or secure area until you redeem it.
